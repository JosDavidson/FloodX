Expert Response:

What is FLEWS?

The Problem Statement

Flood is an annual event in Assam, with over 40% of its land susceptible to damage. While the Central Water Commission (CWC) provided forecasts for major rivers, it did not indicate the specific **areas or villages** where flooding would occur. This left the administrative machinery "clueless" as to which revenue circle should be warned or evacuated.

The FLEWS Solution

FLEWS was developed by the **North Eastern Space Application Centre (NESAC)** in collaboration with **ASDMA** to provide a **location-specific** early warning advisory. It integrates hydro-meteorological data, satellite imagery, and hydrological modeling to issue actionable alerts.

Alert Lead Time

7 to 18 hours

Location Specificity

Revenue Circle / Group of Villages

Success Rate (2012)

Improved to about 65%

Major Technical Components

1. Meteorological Component

Focuses on rainfall prediction and overall probability of rainfall in a basin using numerical models and ground/satellite data.

  • **WRF Model:** Weather Research Forecast model for grid-based rainfall prediction ($3\text{km}$ to $27\text{km}$ resolution).
  • **Synoptic Analysis:** Forecasts based on cloud properties, wind speed, vorticity, and atmospheric conditions.

2. Hydrological Component

Hybrid modeling approach to forecast water discharge and daily river flow hydrographs.

  • **Rational Model (Lumped):** Provides the forecast of the peak discharge value for a river basin.
  • **HEC-HMS Model (Quasi Distributed):** Provides the forecast for the daily hydrograph using SCS-CN method for runoff and Muskingum routing for flow.

Alert Dissemination: User Groups

FLEWS alerts are disseminated to three main user groups via SMS (brief) and Email (detailed with maps) for coordinated response **within Assam**.

Upper Assam Districts

This group primarily covers the eastern and upper reaches of the Brahmaputra valley, focusing on the initial impact zones of upstream rainfall events.

Dhemaji (High Vulnerability)

Lakhimpur (Pilot District, High Vulnerability)

Sivasagar

Alert Cascade Process (ASDMA Flow):

  1. **NESAC** issues SMS/Email alert.
  2. Alert received by **ASDMA Control Room** / **Deputy Commissioners (DC)**.
  3. DC/DPO alerts **Circle Officers (COs)** and **Water Resources Dept.** via SMS/Phone.
  4. COs alert the **Gaon Buras / PRIs / VDM Committees** at the village level for ground action.

Middle Assam & Barak Valley Districts

Alerts for the central region and the southern Barak valley, which includes the Cachar plains region (fed by the Barak River).

Cachar, Hailakandi, Karimganj (Barak Valley Focus)

Darrang, Sonitpur, Morigaon (Central Brahmaputra)

Morigaon and Darrang are identified among the top 5 most flood-prone districts by the FHI.

Alert Cascade Process (ASDMA Flow):

  1. **NESAC** issues SMS/Email alert.
  2. Alert received by **ASDMA Control Room** / **Deputy Commissioners (DC)**.
  3. DC/DPO alerts **Circle Officers (COs)** and **Water Resources Dept.** via SMS/Phone.
  4. COs alert the **Gaon Buras / PRIs / VDM Committees** at the village level for ground action.

Lower Assam Districts

Covers the western and lower areas of the Brahmaputra valley, often impacted by downstream flow accumulation and major tributaries.

Nalbari (Highest Hazard Index - 27)

Barpeta, Baska, Goalpara, Dhubri

These districts are crucial for managing the downstream discharge from the Brahmaputra and its tributaries like the Beki and Manas rivers.

Alert Cascade Process (ASDMA Flow):

  1. **NESAC** issues SMS/Email alert.
  2. Alert received by **ASDMA Control Room** / **Deputy Commissioners (DC)**.
  3. DC/DPO alerts **Circle Officers (COs)** and **Water Resources Dept.** via SMS/Phone.
  4. COs alert the **Gaon Buras / PRIs / VDM Committees** at the village level for ground action.

Wikipedia Analysis: Post-2012 Flood Trends

The Wikipedia data confirms that major catastrophic floods remain a frequent occurrence in Assam (e.g., 2020 and 2022 saw the affected population soar to **5.5 million**). While FLEWS successfully reduced losses in its pilot phase (2010, 2011), the continuing severity of recent floods highlights the need for constant modernization and expansion of the warning system, as recommended in the original project document. The floods are a complex, recurrent issue intensified by the massive discharge from the Brahmaputra River.

Conclusion from the Project Document:

The district administration for those districts under FLEWS has welcomed the early warning and participated in it with great commitment. Sustainability of the project is ensured as it has proved to be an effective tool for decision making.
